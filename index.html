Characterising Exoplanets with CHEOPS

CHEOPS, which stands for CHaracterizing ExOPlanet Satellite, is a s-class satellite launched by ESA to do a follow-up observations of exoplanets with its precision instruments. At Stockholm University I shall mainly work on characterising exoplanets using the observations from CHEOPS mission in order to learn their nature. I shall also assist in developing tools for analysing data from CHEOPS mission.

Spectral pipeline for Phoenix

Phoenix is a high resolution spectrograph at the Gemini Observatory with a bandpass in infrared. Such a waveband (1 to 5 micrometer) is ideal to observe helium in escaping atmosphere of exoplanets. However, being a visiting instrument, it doesn't have a data reduction and spectral extraction pipeline which can reduce raw data. I, along with Dr. Leonardo dos Santos from Geneva Observatory, developed a semi-automatic data reduction and spectral extraction pipeline for Phoenix instrument which is publicly available.

Limb Darkening and TESS

Limb darkening effect plays a crusial role in determining the shape of exoplanet transit light curves. Hence, it is important to take in to account of this effect while modelling exoplanet transit lightcurves --- we can either model for the effect, or use stellar model atmospheres to determine the effect and then used it directly in modelling. With the use of TESS observations of known exoplanets, we have shown that it is not reccommended to use model stellar atmospheres to estimate the limb darkening effect and then fixing it in the modelling of transit lightcurves.

We also provided with the updated planetary parameters of known exoplanets observed by TESS along with the limb darkening coefficients for these planets.

An updated measurements of the Cosmic Star Formation history

Cosmic star formation history is one of the important factor in understanding galaxy growth, its metal enrichment etc. Thus a various attempts have been made to compute it more accurately, more recently by Madau & Dickinson (2014) using the contemporary observations. Since then, in the last decade, a plethora of observations have been made in ultraviolet and in infrared of galaxy population. A luminosity function derived from these observations can be used to find cosmic history of star formation rate density and thus updating the findings of Madau & Dickinson (2014).
